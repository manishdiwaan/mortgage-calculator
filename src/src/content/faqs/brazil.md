  - question: "What is ITBI and how much will I pay?"
    answer: "ITBI (Imposto sobre Transmissão de Bens Imóveis) is a municipal transfer tax charged when property ownership changes hands in Brazil, typically ranging from 2% to 3% of the property's assessed or transaction value, whichever is higher. Each municipality sets its own rate, so rates in São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, and smaller cities can differ. This tax must be paid before the escritura pública is signed and the property is registered, making it one of the first major costs in the purchase process."

  - question: "What is the registro de imóveis and why is it essential?"
    answer: "The registro de imóveis is the official land registry office in Brazil where all property transactions must be formally recorded to have legal effect. Until your purchase is registered at the cartório de registro de imóveis, you are not legally considered the property owner, even if you have signed a purchase agreement and paid in full. Registration provides public notice of ownership and protects buyers against fraud or competing claims. Always verify the property's matrícula document at the registry before completing any purchase."

  - question: "What is an escritura pública and do I always need one?"
    answer: "An escritura pública is a public deed drafted and authenticated by a notary (tabelião) that formally documents a property sale in Brazil. It is mandatory for all cash property purchases but may be replaced by the bank's contract (instrumento particular) when financing through institutions like Caixa Econômica Federal. The escritura must be taken to the cartório de registro de imóveis to complete the transfer of legal title. Notary fees for the escritura are regulated by state and calculated as a percentage of the property value."

  - question: "Who qualifies for Minha Casa Minha Vida?"
    answer: "Minha Casa Minha Vida (MCMV) is Brazil's federal social housing programme targeting low-to-middle-income families, organized into income bands called faixas. Faixa 1 serves families earning up to R$2,640 per month with heavily subsidized units, while higher faixas extend eligibility up to R$8,000 monthly household income. Applicants must not own another property, not have previously received federal housing subsidies, and must be purchasing their first home. The programme offers below-market interest rates and reduced down payments, making homeownership accessible to millions of Brazilians."

  - question: "Can I use my FGTS to buy a property?"
    answer: "Yes, the FGTS (Fundo de Garantia do Tempo de Serviço) is a mandatory severance fund that Brazilian workers can withdraw to assist with property purchases under specific conditions. You may use FGTS as a down payment, to reduce the outstanding balance of an existing SFH mortgage, or to pay up to 80% of monthly installments for a limited period. To qualify, you must have worked at least three years under the CLT regime (accumulated or not), not own property in the municipality where you work or intend to live, and not have an active SFH mortgage elsewhere in Brazil."

  - question: "What is TR indexation and how does it affect my mortgage payments?"
    answer: "TR (Taxa Referencial) is an official Brazilian interest rate index historically used to adjust the outstanding balance of SFH mortgages, effectively meaning your loan principal can increase over time even as you make payments. Combined with a fixed annual interest rate (commonly 8-12% in SFH loans), TR indexation means your installments and remaining balance are recalculated periodically. In recent years, Caixa Econômica Federal introduced IPCA-indexed and fixed-rate mortgage options as alternatives to TR, giving borrowers more predictability. Understanding which index applies to your loan is critical for long-term financial planning."

  - question: "Why does Caixa Econômica Federal dominate the Brazilian mortgage market?"
    answer: "Caixa Econômica Federal is a federal government-owned bank that accounts for roughly 70% of all residential mortgages in Brazil, holding a dominant position unmatched by any single lender in most economies. Caixa administers the FGTS fund and is the primary operator of government housing programmes like Minha Casa Minha Vida, giving it structural advantages in offering subsidized rates. Its mandate to promote social housing and access to credit means it consistently offers rates and terms that private banks struggle to match for lower-income buyers. Many buyers approach Caixa first and compare offers from private banks like Bradesco, Itaú, and Santander before deciding."

  - question: "What is the difference between SFH and SFI financing systems?"
    answer: "SFH (Sistema Financeiro de Habitação) is the regulated housing finance system covering properties valued up to R$1.5 million, offering lower interest rates (capped by law) and allowing use of FGTS funds. SFI (Sistema de Financiamento Imobiliário) covers properties above SFH limits or borrowers who do not meet SFH criteria, with no interest rate cap and no FGTS access, but greater flexibility for lenders and investors. Most first-time homebuyers in Brazil use SFH, while luxury properties and investment purchases typically fall under SFI. Always confirm which system applies to your loan, as it significantly affects your costs and available tools."

  - question: "What is alienação fiduciária and how does it protect the lender?"
    answer: "Alienação fiduciária is Brazil's primary mortgage collateral structure, under which legal title of the property is transferred to the lender (creditor fiduciário) while you retain possession and beneficial ownership as the borrower (devedor fiduciário). If you default on payments, the lender can repossess and auction the property through an extrajudicial process far faster than traditional foreclosure, typically within a few months. This security for lenders has contributed to lower mortgage rates compared to older pledge-based systems. Once you repay the loan in full, title reverts to you and is registered at the cartório de registro de imóveis."

  - question: "What is the habite-se and why do I need it before buying?"
    answer: "The habite-se is an occupancy permit issued by the municipal government certifying that a newly constructed building complies with approved plans and is safe for habitation. Without a valid habite-se, a property cannot be legally occupied, financed through bank mortgages, or have its escritura pública properly registered. Buying a property without this document exposes you to significant legal and financial risk, including the possibility of demolition orders. Always request the habite-se from the developer or seller and verify it with the local prefeitura before signing any purchase contract."

  - question: "Why is my CPF required for every property transaction in Brazil?"
    answer: "The CPF (Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas) is Brazil's individual taxpayer identification number and is mandatory for all parties in any real estate transaction, including buyers, sellers, and spouses. Notaries, registry offices, and banks cannot process deeds, registrations, or mortgage contracts without valid CPFs for all involved parties. Foreign buyers must obtain a CPF from the Receita Federal before purchasing property, which can be done at Brazilian consulates abroad or in-country. Keeping your CPF regular (sem restrições) is essential, as irregularities can block transactions."

  - question: "How much do cartório notary fees cost in Brazil?"
    answer: "Cartório fees in Brazil include charges for drafting the escritura pública at the notary office (tabelionato de notas) and separate registration fees at the cartório de registro de imóveis, both calculated as a percentage of the property's transaction value. Fee tables (emolumentos) are set by each state and typically total between 1% and 2% of the property value when combined, though this varies significantly by state and property price. Some states offer discounts for first-time buyers or properties financed under Minha Casa Minha Vida. Always request a fee simulation from both cartórios before finalizing your budget."

  - question: "What is IPTU and how much should I budget for it?"
    answer: "IPTU (Imposto Predial e Territorial Urbano) is an annual municipal property tax levied on urban real estate in Brazil, charged to the owner as of January 1st each year. Rates typically range from 0.5% to 1.5% of the property's valor venal (municipal assessed value), which is usually lower than market value, though rates and assessments vary widely by municipality and property type. Major cities like São Paulo and Rio de Janeiro have progressive IPTU scales for higher-value properties. You can pay IPTU in a single upfront payment (usually with a discount) or in monthly installments throughout the year."

  - question: "What are condomínio fees and what do they cover?"
    answer: "Condomínio fees (taxa de condomínio) are monthly charges paid by apartment or gated community owners to cover shared building expenses including maintenance, security, cleaning, elevator servicing, insurance, and management costs. In Brazil, these fees can range from a few hundred reais in modest buildings to several thousand reais monthly in luxury condominiums in cities like São Paulo and Rio de Janeiro. Unlike IPTU, condomínio fees are not tax-deductible and can increase unpredictably based on building decisions made by owner assemblies. Before purchasing, request at least 12 months of condomínio statements to assess financial health and any pending special assessments (fundo de reserva extras)."

  - question: "What is a permuta and how does property exchange work in Brazil?"
    answer: "A permuta is a property exchange transaction in which two parties swap properties instead of using cash, a legally recognized and relatively common arrangement in Brazilian real estate. The transaction is formalized through an escritura pública de permuta at a notary, with both properties independently valued to determine if any cash top-up (torna) is required. ITBI is still due on the transaction, calculated on the value of each property being transferred. Permutas are popular among developers who accept existing properties as partial payment for new units, and can offer tax planning advantages in certain situations."

  - question: "How does the Selic rate affect my mortgage payments in Brazil?"
    answer: "The Selic is Brazil's benchmark interest rate set by the Banco Central do Brasil, and it directly influences mortgage costs across all lending institutions. When the Selic rises, variable-rate mortgages indexed to the CDI or market rates become more expensive, while FGTS-linked SFH loans have a statutory cap of 12% per annum that provides some protection. Fixed-rate contracts lock in your cost regardless of Selic movements, making them attractive when rates are high and expected to fall. Monitoring Copom meetings, which set the Selic every 45 days, is essential for timing a mortgage application or refinancing decision."

  - question: "What is the INCC and how does it affect off-plan property purchases?"
    answer: "The INCC, or Índice Nacional de Custo da Construção, measures construction cost inflation in Brazil and is published monthly by the FGV. During the construction phase of an off-plan property, instalments paid to the developer are typically corrected by the INCC, meaning your outstanding balance grows in real terms even as you make payments. In periods of high construction inflation, buyers can see their total purchase price increase significantly above the original contract value. Always model a scenario where INCC runs at 8–12% annually when budgeting for an off-plan purchase."

  - question: "What are my distrato rights if I need to cancel an off-plan purchase contract?"
    answer: "The Lei do Distrato, Law 13.786/2018, regulates the cancellation of off-plan real estate purchase agreements in Brazil and provides specific refund rules depending on whether the development is part of a patrimônio de afetação structure. If the development uses patrimônio de afetação, the developer may retain up to 25% of amounts paid plus a penalty of 0.5% per month for delay in completion if applicable; outside that structure, the retention cap rises to 50%. Refunds under distrato must be paid within 180 days of the contract rescission date. Understanding these rules before signing is critical because cancelling an off-plan contract can result in losing a substantial portion of what you have already paid."

  - question: "What penalty applies if a developer delivers my off-plan property late in Brazil?"
    answer: "Brazilian law and standard incorporation contracts include a built-in tolerance period of up to 180 days beyond the contractually agreed delivery date before a developer is considered to be in default. Once that tolerance window expires, the buyer is entitled to a daily fine typically set at 0.033% of the contract value per day of delay, which corresponds roughly to 1% per month. Some contracts also grant the buyer the right to rescind the contract without penalty and receive a full refund with monetary correction if the delay becomes excessive. Always read the multa por atraso clause carefully and negotiate the highest possible daily rate before signing."

  - question: "What is a loteamento and how does buying a plot in one differ from buying a built property?"
    answer: "A loteamento is a formally approved land subdivision in Brazil where a larger tract is divided into individual plots, or lotes, each receiving its own matricula number at the registro de imóveis. Unlike buying a finished apartment or house, purchasing a lote means you acquire raw land and must separately contract and finance construction, obtain an alvará de construção, and eventually secure a habite-se before the property can be legally occupied or mortgaged as a built dwelling. Loteamentos must be approved by the municipal authority and registered under Lei 6.766/1979, so always confirm registration status before paying any reservation fee. Infrastructure obligations such as roads, drainage, and utilities are legally required of the developer but delivery timelines can be unpredictable."

Under Lei 6.530/1978, only individuals holding a valid CRECI registration in the relevant state may legally broker property transactions and earn commission, and working with an unlicensed intermediary exposes both buyer and seller to legal risk. You can verify an agent's CRECI number and current status on the council's online portal for each state, which takes only a few minutes. A legitimate CRECI holder carries professional liability and is subject to disciplinary proceedings, providing an important layer of consumer protection."

  - question: "What rental yields can I expect from investment property in São Paulo and Rio de Janeiro?"
    answer: "Gross rental yields in São Paulo typically range from 4% to 7% annually, with compact studio and one-bedroom units in high-demand neighbourhoods such as Pinheiros, Vila Madalena, and Itaim Bibi performing toward the upper end of that range. Rio de Janeiro yields are broadly similar at 4% to 6.5%, though tourist-oriented short-term rental properties in Ipanema and Leblon can achieve higher gross returns but with greater vacancy and regulatory risk. Net yields after condomínio fees, IPTU, property management costs, and occasional vacancy typically run 1.5 to 2.5 percentage points below the gross figure. Comparing net yield against the Selic rate is the standard local benchmark for deciding whether a property investment makes financial sense."

  - question: "What is the GCAP tax on capital gains from property sales and what exemptions apply?"
    answer: "Capital gains on real estate sold in Brazil are subject to Imposto de Renda sobre Ganho de Capital, commonly called GCAP, at a progressive rate ranging from 15% on gains up to R$5 million to 22.5% on gains exceeding R$30 million. The most important exemption allows sellers to exclude 100% of the gain if the sale value does not exceed R$440,000 and the seller has not used any property exemption in the preceding five years. A second major exemption applies when the full sale proceeds are reinvested in another residential property within 180 days of the deed date. Long-term ownership also benefits from a reducing factor called fator de redução that gradually lowers the taxable gain for properties held for many years."

  - question: "What rules apply when a foreign buyer wants to remit funds into Brazil to purchase property?"
    answer: "Foreign buyers must channel purchase funds through the Brazilian financial system via an authorised institution and register the inward remittance as a foreign capital entry with the Banco Central do Brasil using the RDE-IED or RDE-Portfolio electronic systems depending on the transaction structure. Registration is essential because it is the legal basis for repatriating the investment proceeds or future sale gains back to the buyer's home country without excessive bureaucratic obstacles. The foreign buyer must hold a valid CPF and typically opens a non-resident bank account in Brazil to receive the converted reais. Exchange rate risk is real because the purchase contract and mortgage, if any, are denominated in reais, and BRL/USD or BRL/EUR volatility can materially affect the effective cost of the acquisition."

  - question: "What are FIIs and are they a good alternative to buying direct property in Brazil?"
    answer: "Fundos de Investimento Imobiliário, or FIIs, are Brazilian real estate investment funds traded on the B3 stock exchange that allow retail investors to access diversified commercial property portfolios, receivables, or mortgage-backed assets with as little as R$100. Income distributions from FIIs are currently exempt from income tax for individual investors holding less than 10% of fund units when the fund has at least 50 unitholders and is publicly traded, making them tax-efficient compared to direct rental income. FIIs offer liquidity that direct property cannot, allowing investors to buy or sell positions daily, but they carry market price volatility and manager risk. For foreign buyers or those without local property management capacity, FIIs can be a practical and regulated entry point to Brazilian real estate exposure."

  - question: "How does the Selic rate influence the decision between a FGTS-linked mortgage and a market-rate mortgage?"
    answer: "FGTS-linked mortgages under the SFH system carry an interest rate capped at 12% per annum plus TR, making them relatively attractive when the Selic is high because market-rate mortgages from private banks are priced at spreads above the CDI, which closely tracks the Selic. When the Selic is at 10% or above, the effective cost of a private bank mortgage can easily reach 13–16% per annum, widening the gap significantly in favour of FGTS or Caixa SFH products. However, SFH eligibility requires the buyer to meet FGTS usage criteria and the property to fall within the SFH value limits, which are updated periodically by the Banco Central. Comparing the CET, or Custo Efetivo Total, which includes all fees and insurance, across lenders is the most reliable way to evaluate the true cost of each option."

  - question: "How does mortgage portabilidade work and can it save me money in Brazil?"
    answer: "Portabilidade de crédito imobiliário allows you to transfer your existing mortgage to another bank that offers a lower interest rate, without paying the loan off and taking a new one, preserving your original contract conditions such as FGTS eligibility. The receiving bank must match or beat your current total effective cost (CET), and your current lender has the right to match the offer within five business days before the transfer proceeds. The process is regulated by the Banco Central do Brasil and is free of IOF tax, though you may still face cartório fees for updating the alienação fiduciária registration. Comparing CET rather than just the nominal rate is essential, as some banks offset lower rates with higher insurance or administrative charges."

  - question: "Is seguro incêndio property insurance compulsory for a Brazilian mortgage and what does it cover?"
    answer: "Yes, seguro incêndio is legally mandatory for all mortgages financed under the SFH system and is typically required by lenders under SFI contracts as well, covering the physical structure of the property against fire, lightning, and explosion at minimum. The premium is usually bundled into your monthly mortgage instalment and calculated on the reconstruction value of the property, not its market value. Borrowers have the right under Banco Central rules to contract the insurance independently from the lender, which can sometimes reduce the cost compared to the bank's tied product. Contents insurance and civil liability cover are separate and optional, but strongly recommended for tenanted or high-value properties."

  - question: "What are my rights if I have a dispute with my condomínio síndico in Brazil?"
    answer: "The síndico is the elected manager of a condomínio and is bound by the Lei do Condomínio (Law 4,591/1964) and the condomínio's internal regimento interno, meaning decisions that exceed their mandate or violate those rules can be challenged by any unit owner. You can call an extraordinary assembléia geral by collecting signatures from at least one quarter of the unit owners to vote on removing the síndico or reversing an unlawful decision. Disputes over accounts, unauthorized spending, or discriminatory fines can also be taken to a mediação extrajudicial or, if unresolved, to the civil courts, where judges regularly rule against síndicos who act outside their statutory authority. Keeping written records of all communications and attending assemblies is the most practical way to protect your position as a unit owner."

Rio de Janeiro's market is more segmented, with the South Zone maintaining premium values in Leblon, Ipanema, and Lagoa, while the Barra da Tijuca corridor appeals to buyers seeking larger units at comparatively lower prices. Both cities saw real price growth accelerate after 2022 as housing supply tightened relative to demand, though high Selic rates have moderated transaction volumes in the broader market.     answer: "Brazil does not yet have a single federal law specifically regulating short-term platforms like Airbnb, but the Superior Tribunal de Justiça ruled in 2021 that condomínios have the right to prohibit short-term rentals through a deliberação in their assembléia geral, so the first step is checking your condomínio's regimento interno. Rental income from short-term lets is taxable as renda de pessoa física and must be declared monthly via the Carnê-Leão system and annually in your Imposto de Renda, with rates up to 27.5 percent applying above exemption thresholds. Municipal tourism regulations in cities like São Paulo, Rio, and Florianópolis are evolving and may require registration as a hospedagem não hoteleira or compliance with health and safety inspections. Platform liability and guest protection rules continue to develop, making legal advice and adequate insurance coverage essential for hosts with multiple units."

  - question: "How does the processo sucessório work when inheriting property in Brazil?"
    answer: "When a property owner dies in Brazil, the estate must pass through inventário, either by formal judicial process or, if all heirs are adults and there is a valid will or agreement, by the faster extrajudicial route via a cartório notary office under Law 11,441/2007. The ITCMD inheritance tax is levied by the state where the property is located at rates that vary between roughly 2 percent and 8 percent depending on the state, with São Paulo currently charging a flat 4 percent and Rio de Janeiro applying a progressive scale. Heirs cannot sell or mortgage inherited property until the formal transfer of title is completed and registered at the registro de imóveis, which means illiquid periods of months or even years are common if heirs dispute the division. Appointing an inventariante, the administrator of the estate, early and ensuring the deceased's CPF and property registration are fully regularized significantly reduces delays and costs."
