  - question: "What are the AVD stamp duty rates for permanent residents buying their first property in Hong Kong?"
    answer: "Hong Kong Permanent Residents (HKPR) purchasing their first residential property pay Ad Valorem Duty (AVD) at the lower Scale 2 rates, ranging from HK$100 for properties under HK$3 million up to 4.25% for properties above HK$21.739 million. If the HKPR already owns any residential property at the time of purchase, they must pay the higher Scale 1 rates, which are a flat 15% of the purchase price. It is critical to confirm your HKPR status and existing property holdings before signing any Provisional Agreement for Sale and Purchase."

This effectively doubles the transaction cost compared to a first-time HKPR buyer and is a major financial consideration for overseas purchasers. The only exemption is if a non-permanent resident later acquires HKPR status and sells the property, a refund mechanism exists under specific conditions."

This includes foreign nationals, expatriates on work visas, and Mainland Chinese citizens regardless of their income or residency duration in Hong Kong. Companies purchasing residential property, even those incorporated in Hong Kong, are also subject to BSD, making corporate ownership structures generally unattractive for residential purchases."

  - question: "What is the Special Stamp Duty (SSD) and how does it affect property resale in Hong Kong?"
    answer: "Special Stamp Duty (SSD) is a resale tax introduced to discourage short-term property speculation, applying to residential properties resold within 36 months of acquisition. The SSD rate is 20% if resold within 6 months, 15% between 6 and 12 months, and 10% between 12 and 36 months. Properties held beyond 36 months are exempt from SSD, making it a significant deterrent for investors who intended to flip properties quickly."

The refund brings the effective duty down to the Scale 2 rates applicable to a first-time buyer. The refund application must be submitted to the Inland Revenue Department with supporting documentation confirming the disposal of the original property."

  - question: "What are the HKMA LTV limits for residential mortgages in Hong Kong?"
    answer: "The Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA) imposes maximum loan-to-value (LTV) ratios that vary based on property value and borrower type. Self-occupied properties valued at HK$10 million or below can access up to 70% LTV, while those valued between HK$10 million and HK$30 million are capped at 60% LTV, and properties above HK$30 million are limited to 50% LTV. Non-owner-occupied properties and those purchased by buyers with income primarily sourced outside Hong Kong attract lower LTV caps, typically 10 percentage points lower."

  - question: "How does the HKMA stress test work for mortgage applicants in Hong Kong?"
    answer: "The HKMA requires banks to apply a stress test whereby the borrower's debt-servicing ratio (DSR) must remain below 60% even if mortgage rates rise by 2 percentage points above the current offered rate. In practice, this means most borrowers must demonstrate sufficient monthly income to cover a hypothetically higher repayment, significantly limiting how much they can borrow relative to their income. The stress test applies to all regulated bank mortgages and is a key reason many applicants find their borrowing capacity lower than expected."
    category: "financing"
  - question: "What is the debt-servicing ratio (DSR) limit for Hong Kong mortgage applicants?"
    answer: "Under HKMA guidelines, the standard debt-servicing ratio limit is 50% of a borrower's gross monthly income, meaning total monthly debt repayments including the new mortgage cannot exceed half of gross income. For borrowers whose primary income source is outside Hong Kong, the DSR limit is further reduced to 40%. Lenders calculate DSR using the stressed interest rate (current rate plus 2%), making it important to have a clean credit profile and minimise other outstanding debts before applying."

  - question: "What is a HIBOR-linked mortgage in Hong Kong and how does it work?"
    answer: "A HIBOR-linked mortgage charges interest at the Hong Kong Interbank Offered Rate (HIBOR) plus a fixed spread, most commonly one-month HIBOR plus approximately 1.3% to 1.5%. Because HIBOR fluctuates daily based on interbank lending conditions, your effective mortgage rate and monthly repayment can change every month. Most HIBOR mortgages include a cap linked to the Prime rate (P-rate) to protect borrowers from extreme rate spikes, typically capped at Prime minus 2% to 2.5%."

  - question: "What is a Prime rate (P-rate) mortgage in Hong Kong and how is it structured?"
    answer: "A Prime rate mortgage charges interest at a fixed discount below the bank's Best Lending Rate, commonly quoted as P minus 2% to P minus 3%, where P currently stands at 5.875% for major banks as of 2024. Unlike HIBOR mortgages, the rate only changes when the bank formally adjusts its Prime rate, which happens infrequently and usually follows the US Federal Reserve's rate decisions. P-rate mortgages offer more payment stability but may carry a slightly higher effective rate than HIBOR mortgages during periods of low interbank rates."

HIBOR mortgages historically offered lower effective rates during stable or low-rate environments but expose borrowers to monthly fluctuations, whereas P-rate mortgages provide predictability since changes are less frequent and require a formal bank decision. Most Hong Kong mortgage packages allow borrowers to switch between HIBOR and P-rate after an initial lock-in period, giving flexibility to adapt to changing market conditions."

Eligible buyers must meet income and asset limits set by the government and do not own any residential property in Hong Kong. Allocation is determined by a computerised ballot, with priority given to Green Form applicants (public housing tenants) over White Form applicants (private market residents), and successful applicants purchase flats at a discounted price with a land premium payable only upon resale in the open market."

White Form applicants are non-public housing residents who meet the income and asset eligibility criteria and compete in a separate ballot with a smaller quota of available flats. Green Form buyers also benefit from higher mortgage LTV ratios under the Housing Authority's endorsed mortgage schemes compared to White Form buyers."

  - question: "How do I calculate the rental yield on a Hong Kong investment property?"
    answer: "Gross rental yield is calculated by dividing annual rental income by the purchase price and multiplying by 100; for example, a property purchased at HK$6 million generating HK$18,000 per month yields a gross figure of 3.6%. Net rental yield deducts ongoing costs including management fees, rates, landlord insurance, agent commissions, and vacancy periods, which in Hong Kong typically reduce gross yield by 1–1.5 percentage points. Hong Kong residential yields have historically ranged between 2% and 3.5% net, which is relatively low by global standards, meaning capital appreciation has traditionally driven total returns. When comparing yields, ensure you use saleable area rather than gross floor area to make consistent comparisons across listings."

However, the Special Stamp Duty effectively acts as a penalty on short-term resale within 36 months of purchase, functioning as a deterrent to speculative flipping rather than a tax on gains.   - question: "How much stamp duty must I pay on a tenancy agreement in Hong Kong?"
    answer: "Tenancy agreements in Hong Kong are subject to stamp duty at rates based on the annual rent and lease term: for leases not exceeding one year the duty is 0.25% of total rent, for leases of one to three years it is 0.5% of average annual rent, and for leases exceeding three years it is 1% of average annual rent. Both landlord and tenant are jointly liable to pay the stamp duty, though in practice it is typically shared equally or negotiated between the parties. Unstamped tenancy agreements cannot be admitted as evidence in court proceedings, which weakens both parties' legal position in any dispute. Stamping must be completed within 30 days of execution if the agreement is signed in Hong Kong, or 30 days after receipt in Hong Kong if signed overseas."

    answer: "A reverse mortgage allows homeowners aged 55 or above to borrow against the equity in their owned property while continuing to live in it, receiving monthly payouts rather than making repayments. The HKMC administers the Reverse Mortgage Programme, which is available for properties valued up to HK$25 million and loans can be structured as fixed-term or lifetime payout options. Interest accrues on the outstanding loan balance and is repaid from the property sale proceeds after the borrower permanently vacates or passes away, with any remaining equity returned to the estate. Eligible properties include private residential properties and HOS flats with premium fully paid, and the homeowner must hold clear title."

    answer: "A conveyancing solicitor in Hong Kong handles all legal aspects of the property transfer, including reviewing the title deeds, conducting requisitions on title, preparing the formal sale and purchase agreement, and lodging stamp duty with the Inland Revenue Department. They also liaise with the Land Registry to register the new ownership and coordinate with your bank's solicitors to ensure mortgage funds are released on the completion date.   - question: "What is a provisional sale and purchase agreement and what are my obligations once I sign it in Hong Kong?"
    answer: "A provisional sale and purchase agreement (PSPA) is a binding preliminary contract signed between buyer and seller in Hong Kong, typically prepared by the estate agent, that commits both parties to the transaction immediately upon signing. At signing, the buyer pays an initial deposit of around 3% to 5% of the purchase price, which the seller may forfeit or sue to recover if the buyer defaults, and equally the seller must refund the deposit and pay an equivalent penalty if they withdraw. The formal sale and purchase agreement, drafted by solicitors, is usually signed within 14 days of the PSPA and the remaining deposit is paid at that stage, making it critical to have financing approved before committing to the PSPA."

  - question: "How is property tax calculated on rental income in Hong Kong and who must pay it?"
    answer: "Property tax in Hong Kong is levied on the net assessable value of a property that is let out, calculated as the actual rent received minus a statutory allowance of 20% for repairs and outgoings, with the resulting amount taxed at a flat rate of 15%. Owners who are individuals may elect to have their rental income assessed under personal assessment instead, which can be advantageous if their overall tax position benefits from deductions such as mortgage interest. Corporate owners are generally not subject to property tax but pay profits tax on rental income instead, so the choice of ownership structure can significantly affect the total tax burden on an investment property."
