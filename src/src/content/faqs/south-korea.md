- question: "How does capital gains tax (yangdo soduk se) work in South Korea?"
  answer: "Yangdo soduk se is levied on the profit made when selling real estate, with basic rates ranging from 6% to 45% aligned with general income tax brackets, but heavy surcharges apply to multi-home owners or those selling within short holding periods. Selling within one year of purchase attracts a 70% rate, and selling within two years attracts 60%, while short-term sales in regulated zones face additional penalties. One-household one-home owners who have held the property for at least two years and meet residency requirements may qualify for full exemption up to KRW 1.2 billion in gains. The government adjusts these rates periodically as part of housing market stabilisation policy, so confirming the current rate with a tax accountant before selling is strongly advised."
  category: "costs"

- question: "What is property holding tax (jaesan se) in South Korea?"
  answer: "Jaesan se, or property tax, is a local government tax assessed twice yearly in July and September on all real estate including land, buildings, and housing. Rates range from 0.1% to 0.4% for residential property depending on the official assessed value, and the tax base is the publicly announced price multiplied by a fair market value ratio set annually by the government. Unlike jonghap budongsan se, jaesan se applies to all property owners regardless of total portfolio value, making it a universal holding cost. Local governments use the revenue to fund municipal services, and owners of multiple properties will receive separate bills for each asset."
